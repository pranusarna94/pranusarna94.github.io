Grab Food merchant app

The brief

Build a channel which lets merchants action upon an order made by the customers on the Grab Food app.

Understanding the users

To understand the restaurant operations I visited 3 restaurants in my city during lunch time and was fortunate enough to witness these restaurants at their peak order volume. Being a fly on the wall for these restaurants taught me a pattern in which restaurants generally work in and I believe this pattern is more or less the same throughout the world.

How restaurant generally work

Through my visits to these restaurants, I also tried to understand the users for this app. Here is what I observed. Constantly increasing new orders, customers calling to know the status of or to make changes to their orders, back and forth with the kitchen to pass the new orders or check up on the old ones, keeping a track of the delivery executives, managing inventory. Their job was chaotic and tough!

Restaurant managers are busy people

Defining the problem(s)

On understanding the managers and the circumstances they operate in, I was in a better position to define the goals for the app. Here they go:

  1. The app should give clear visibility of in-kitchen orders, incoming new orders and past orders. It should also let the manager action upon these orders.
  2. The app should ensure that the major task of searching and identifying an order is easy and quick.

The solution

In kitchen orders

Orders which are currently being prepared are housed in the first tab of the app. This is done as in kitchen orders require more attention and immediate actioning.

Orders which need immediate actioning are shown upfront

Incoming new orders

The new orders button is a gloabl level element which means it can draw over other apps. This button is designed to draw attention of the users but at the same time not distract them from a task which they might already be performing on the phone. This is done by momentarily ringing the phone (to draw attention if user is away from phone) and by making the button flash by inverting its colour (to draw attention when the user is using the phone).

The "New Order" button flashes by inverting colour to draw attention.

On accepting an order, the manager is presented with a pop-up in which they can change the preparation time for the order. This is done as the managers themselves have the best idea about the physical reality of their kitchen and this time can be used by the Grab system to precisely allot a delivery rider for the order.

Once the order is accepted, it is appended at the bottom of the list and the view window is scrolled to that order. This is done to make sure that the restaurant manager adds this order to their POS and passes it on to the kitchen. The managers can quicly view the order details which further aids in the process of adding the order to the POS.

Rejecting an order

The manager may reject an order due to many reasons. Two reasons which stood out the most when I was talking to the managers were "Items out of stock" and "Kitchen overloaded". I tried to think of ways which would help the restaurant cut down on rejections.

Items out of stock

The item which is marked as out of stock, is taken off the menu in the customer app. This will make sure that rejection don't happen due to these items in the future.

When the restaurant is facing high order volume we give them some breathing room.

When the restaurant is facing higher order volume we give them a breathing room by queing up orders and sending them to the restaurant when the breathing room is over. The time period of 10 minutes is based on intuition and can be finely tweaked through experimentation.

The customers are made aware about this delay on their apps.

Setting the right expectations with the customers.

Making search and identification of orders easier

Since managers are busy people every second of their time is valuable. Searching and identifaction of an order was important for the managers to communicate about it with their kitchen, the Grab rider and Grab support. This was done by showing the orders which matter the most upfront.

The restaurant managers communicate with their kitchen about an order using the order items. Hence, to aid this communication between the managers and the kitchen it was important to surface the items on the order card.

The order card makes it easier to identify an order.

The search bar on the active orders tab helps the manager quickly search for an order by the order-id (when communicating with support) or by the rider name (when the rider comes to pickup order).

Managers may search for an order using the rider name or the order ID

Other screens and flows

Grab rider reached the restaurant

Once a Grab rider reaches the restaurant, the order card for that rider changes to "Rider reached" and is added to the top of the list as this order now needs immediate actioning. The manager identifes the rider based on their name. Once the order is dispatched, it moves from the "Preparing" tab to the "All orders" tab.

Managers handing over the orders to the riders.

Making managers realise the implications of a delayed order

To make sure that a manager does not mindlessly keep adding time for preparation, a warning sign is surfaced once they go over a certain threshold.

Warning surfaced to make sure that managers don't mindlessly keep adding time.

All orders

The all orders has all the orders made to the restaurant till date and can be filtered based on time and the status of the order.

The "All orders" tab

The menu tab

Another insight from my visits to the restaurants was how dynamic their menu was. As the items kept running out of inventory it was important to give the restaurant managers a quick way to mark items out of stock. The managers can search for items through their menu and mark them back in stock as well.

The "Menu" tab

Learnings

This experience of crafting an app for restaurant managers made me realise how important and tough their job is. Also, indulging in even a little bit of user research makes the final design better by miles as that makes you empathise with the users and helps understand their problem better. If I had a little more time on my hands I would have loved to understand the usability issues with the app by doing some scenario based guirella user testing. Prototyping the animations for the "New Order" and "Order dispatched" flows would have come second.
